title: Must / Can't

## Learning Goal
Use **must** and **can't** for logical deduction about present and past situations.

## Core Grammar
**Must** means you are almost sure something is true. **Can't** means you are almost sure something is not true. For past deduction, use **must have** or **can't have**.

## Examples
- She must be tired after the test.
- This answer can't be correct.
- He must have misunderstood the question.

## Jason's Confusing Points
- **Mustn't** means prohibition, not negative deduction.
- Negative deduction uses **can't**, not **mustn't**.

## Wrong Answers
- Wrong: She mustn't be at home. The lights are off.
- Correct: She can't be at home. The lights are off.
- Wrong: He must has forgotten.
- Correct: He must have forgotten.

## One-line Summary
Use **must** when you are sure yes, and **can't** when you are sure no.
